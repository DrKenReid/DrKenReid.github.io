The Prevention Paradox: Why Success Is Invisible

19 May 2026 · personal

There's a surgeon who operated brilliantly on a patient who was in a car crash. Everyone heard about it — the news covered it, the family sent a card, the surgeon got a commendation. There's also a traffic engineer who quietly redesigned an intersection ten years ago. Nobody heard much about that work, because the crash it helped prevent never happened.

We have a consistent bias toward dramatic success and against quiet preventive success. The firefighter gets the parade; the person who installed the sprinkler system gets a line in a maintenance log. The doctor who treated the outbreak is on the front page; the epidemiologist who caught it before it spread is buried in the acknowledgements. This is structural, and once you see it, it's hard to stop noticing it everywhere.

A quick glossary

  • Prevention paradox: a term from epidemiology (Rose, 1985) describing how a measure that benefits a population may offer little felt benefit to any individual — making it hard to justify politically or personally.
  • Counterfactual: what would have happened if a given action hadn't been taken. Prevention often shows up only in the counterfactual, which is by definition unseen.
  • Negativity bias: the tendency to weight negative events and outcomes more heavily than positive ones of equivalent magnitude.
  • Availability heuristic: the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind — which favours dramatic, memorable events over quiet, routine ones.

Absence is hard to count

This is the fundamental problem with prevention. When something goes wrong, there is an event: a signal, a crisis, a news story, a person to blame or praise. When something is prevented, there is usually only the absence of an event, and the human brain is not especially good at noticing absences.

Daniel Kahneman's work on cognitive biases is relevant here. The availability heuristic (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973) describes how people estimate the probability and importance of events based on how easily they come to mind. A plane crash comes to mind easily; the millions of safe flights don't. A violent crime features on the news; the thousands of interactions that did not turn violent never enter the record. Prevention, by its nature, has little to display. The counterfactual — the pandemic that did not happen, the accident that did not occur, the building that did not burn down — remains outside ordinary perception.

There's also what epidemiologist Geoffrey Rose described in 1985 as the prevention paradox: a preventive measure that benefits a whole population may produce no obvious benefit for most individuals who experience it (Rose, 1985). You wash your hands and, most days, you were probably not going to get sick anyway. The benefit is real in aggregate but hard to feel personally, so people rarely feel grateful for it, and they rarely reward the people who made it happen.

The examples are persuasive, but messy

Handwashing is the obvious one. Ignaz Semmelweis figured out in the 1840s that doctors washing their hands between the morgue and the delivery ward would dramatically cut maternal mortality. He was right, and he was ignored, ridiculed, and eventually committed to an asylum, where he died of the very infection he had been trying to prevent (Nuland, 2003). His idea was demonstrably correct, but it asked people to change their behaviour to stop something that had no accepted cause at the time. Before germ theory was widely accepted, there was no pathogen to point at; there was only a correlation in the data and a recommendation that sounded, to the doctors who were supposed to be the experts, like an accusation.

Road safety works the same way. The UK's road fatality rate fell from around 7,500 deaths per year in the early 1970s to under 1,700 by 2020 (Department for Transport, 2021). That's a massive change — thousands of lives saved every year — achieved through a combination of seatbelt legislation, speed limits, drink-driving laws, road design improvements, and vehicle safety standards. Yet there are no ceremonies for the people who lobbied for seatbelt legislation in the 1960s and 1970s, and no memorials to the engineers who designed safer road layouts. The work and the lives saved were real, but the credit arrives diffusely, if at all.

Y2K is a useful case study precisely because it is not as clean as people often make it. In the late 1990s, thousands of programmers and IT professionals worked for years to audit and fix systems that used two-digit year formats, which could have caused failures when clocks rolled over to 2000. Some of the warnings were sober; some were inflated by consultants and publishers with an obvious interest in alarm (Yourdon & Yourdon, 1998). In hindsight, many unfixed systems would probably have produced minor glitches rather than catastrophic failures, while other systems really did need careful remediation. When 1 January 2000 arrived and little went wrong, a large part of the cultural response became mockery: proof, supposedly, that the whole thing had been a lucrative panic. The harder truth is less satisfying. Some of the calm was bought by serious preventive work, and some of the panic probably oversold the danger. Even successful prevention can leave behind an argument about whether it was necessary, excessive, or both.

In public health, vaccines are the canonical example. Vaccination eliminated smallpox, a disease that killed an estimated 300 million people in the 20th century alone (Fenner et al., 1988). Because smallpox no longer exists in the general population, it is now an abstract historical fact rather than a visceral present threat. The result is that the danger is systematically underestimated, and the preventive measure — vaccination — faces resistance that the original disease, were people still dying from it, would almost certainly not face. When prevention works well enough, it can erase its own justification from people's lived experience.

The cognitive science underpinning this is well-established. Beyond the availability heuristic, there's the related concept of negativity bias: we weight negative events more heavily than positive ones of equivalent magnitude (Baumeister et al., 2001). A near miss feels more significant than a routine safe journey. A contaminated water supply makes headlines in a way that the preceding years of clean supply do not. This asymmetry means that reactive responses to present harm get more attention, more funding, and more political will than proactive responses to potential harm.

There's also an attribution problem. When something goes wrong, there's a clear causal chain: something happened, someone was responsible, here is the outcome. When something is prevented, the causal chain runs through a counterfactual. The traffic cone sits in the road, the driver swerves, and no one is hurt, but the cone and the person who placed it are rarely credited for the non-crash. The absence of injury becomes part of the normal, unremarkable texture of a day that went fine.

In organisational settings, this creates what some management researchers call the "hero vs. guardian" dynamic (Taleb, 2012). The person who averts a crisis before it becomes public is rarely rewarded. The person who responds after the crisis breaks often is, even if the emergency was preventable, and even if the heroic response was only necessary because preventive work wasn't done or wasn't funded. Nassim Taleb makes this point sharply in Antifragile: a politician who prevents a war gets none of the credit that a general who wins one receives, even though preventing it would have been the better outcome by any reasonable measure.

The pattern also shows up in ordinary professional life. The software engineer who catches a security vulnerability before deployment gets little fanfare, because the system just keeps working; the one who fixes the breach after it happens gets war stories and a reputation. The editor who cuts the paragraph that would have caused a complaint is unseen; the editor who misses it gets the angry email. The accountant who spots the discrepancy before it becomes a problem gets a nod; the one who handles the audit after it became a problem gets a starring role.

I work in data science, and this dynamic comes up in subtle ways. A model that quietly prevents bad recommendations — filtering out outputs that would have caused harm — is never celebrated, because the bad recommendations just don't appear. The model that generates the flashy result, the chart that gets into the boardroom presentation, the prediction that was dramatically right — that's what gets noticed. The guard rail is structurally ignored by anyone who isn't specifically looking for it.

The same is true in personal life. Driving defensively — keeping space, anticipating what other drivers might do, slowing down before the situation demands it — means you avoid incidents that a less attentive driver might have had. But you can't point to those incidents, because they didn't happen. You just arrive safely, unremarkably, again and again, which is exactly the point and exactly why it is taken for granted.

The harder political question

The practical consequences are significant. Preventive health gets systematically underfunded relative to treatment. Mental health prevention receives a fraction of what mental health crisis intervention receives, despite evidence that early intervention is cheaper and more effective (Knapp et al., 2011). Infrastructure maintenance — the kind that keeps bridges from failing, water systems from contaminating, power grids from going down — is perennially deprioritised in favour of new-build projects, because maintenance preserves the status quo while new builds create tangible evidence of activity and investment.

Pandemic preparedness is the most politically vivid example. After SARS in 2003 and H1N1 in 2009, funding for pandemic preparedness increased. As memories faded and the threat became abstract, funding was cut again. When COVID-19 hit, many governments were caught without stockpiles, without plans, without infrastructure, because the preparation had been allowed to atrophy. The people who had warned this would happen — who had written the reports, run the simulations, made the recommendations — were largely ignored until the moment that their predictions came true, at which point the crisis demanded a reactive response rather than the proactive one they had been advocating for (Gates, 2015).

That example can make the lesson seem simpler than it is. Expert warnings do not arrive with labels attached: this one is COVID-19, this one is a false alarm, this one is a real risk that will be softened by luck or by systems already in place. Governments, organisations, and the public all develop warning fatigue when serious people repeatedly describe plausible disasters that never quite arrive. The "boy who cried wolf" problem is not just a failure of attention; it is part of the environment in which prevention has to operate.

There is also no such thing as infinite prevention. Every pound spent hardening one system is a pound not spent on another, and every regulation that removes one risk may introduce delay, cost, or friction somewhere else. We cannot prevent every crash, infection, breach, flood, fire, or failure. A society that tried to drive all risk to zero would spend itself into paralysis. The real argument is usually harder than "prevention good, reaction bad"; it is about which future harms are probable enough, severe enough, and preventable enough to justify present sacrifice.

This isn't irrational in the narrow sense. Politicians respond to constituents with immediate needs. Voters punish inaction on present problems and often don't reward proactive action on future ones. The incentive structures push systematically toward reaction and away from prevention, but knowing that the bias exists doesn't dissolve the budget constraint, the uncertainty, or the politics.

Still, there are ways to see the work more honestly. Public health bodies can track "deaths averted" rather than only deaths. Engineering teams can document near-misses and the interventions that prevented them. Organisations can recognise the people who stopped problems as well as the people who solved them after the fact. None of this turns uncertainty into certainty, but it does make the accounting less one-sided.

There is a personal version of this too: noticing the preventive work around you before it becomes part of the furniture. The colleague who read the brief carefully and caught the error. The parent who planned the route before the trip and avoided the traffic. The developer who wrote the test that caught the regression. These things have little drama and often no story, but they are a large part of what makes a competent life look uneventful.

Geoffrey Rose (1985) put it well: the prevention paradox means that the apparent beneficiaries of a mass preventive measure are few, while the hidden beneficiaries are many. That asymmetry doesn't mean every warning is right or every intervention is worth the cost. Some alarms will be false, some safeguards will be excessive, and some risks will only look obvious after the fact. But the value is still there, scattered through all the uneventful days we are tempted to treat as automatic. The difficulty is that human beings are always being asked to care about the road not taken, and we are never going to find that easy.
