The Prevention Paradox: Why Success Is Invisible

19 May 2026 · personal

There's a surgeon who operated brilliantly on a patient who was in a car crash. Everyone heard about it — the news covered it, the family sent a card, the surgeon got a commendation. There's also a traffic engineer who quietly redesigned an intersection ten years ago. Nobody heard about that one. The crash never happened.

We have a consistent bias toward visible, dramatic success and against invisible preventive success. The firefighter gets the parade; the person who installed the sprinkler system gets nothing. The doctor who treated the outbreak is on the front page; the epidemiologist who caught it before it spread is in a footnote. This isn't random. It's structural, and once you see it, it's hard to stop noticing it everywhere.

A quick glossary

  • Prevention paradox: a term from epidemiology (Rose, 1985) describing how a measure that benefits a population may offer little visible benefit to any individual — making it hard to justify politically or personally.
  • Counterfactual: what would have happened if a given action hadn't been taken. Prevention only shows up in the counterfactual, which is by definition invisible.
  • Negativity bias: the tendency to weight negative events and outcomes more heavily than positive ones of equivalent magnitude.
  • Availability heuristic: the tendency to judge the likelihood of an event based on how easily examples come to mind — which favours dramatic, memorable events over quiet, routine ones.

You don't notice what doesn't happen

This is the fundamental problem with prevention. When something goes wrong, there's an event — a signal, a crisis, a news story, a person to blame or praise. When something is prevented, there is no event. There's just an absence of one. And the human brain is not wired to notice absences.

Daniel Kahneman's work on cognitive biases is relevant here. The availability heuristic (Kahneman & Tversky, 1973) describes how people estimate the probability and importance of events based on how easily they come to mind. A plane crash comes to mind easily; the millions of safe flights don't. A violent crime features on the news; the thousands of interactions that didn't turn violent don't register. Prevention, by its nature, has nothing to show for itself. The counterfactual — the pandemic that didn't happen, the accident that didn't occur, the building that didn't burn down — is structurally invisible.

There's also what epidemiologist Geoffrey Rose described in 1985 as the prevention paradox: a preventive measure that benefits a whole population may produce no visible benefit for most individuals who experience it (Rose, 1985). You wash your hands. You probably weren't going to get sick anyway. The benefit is real in aggregate but invisible to you personally. So people don't feel grateful for it. They don't even notice it. And they certainly don't reward the people who made it happen.

The examples are everywhere once you look

Handwashing is the obvious one. Ignaz Semmelweis figured out in the 1840s that doctors washing their hands between the morgue and the delivery ward would dramatically cut maternal mortality. He was right. He was also ignored, ridiculed, and eventually committed to an asylum, where he died of the very infection he had been trying to prevent (Nuland, 2003). His idea was correct, demonstrably so, and the reward he got was institutional rejection. The reason is straightforward: he was asking people to change their behaviour to prevent something that had no visible cause. There was no germ to point at. There was no drama. There was just a correlation he'd noticed in the data, and a recommendation that felt like an insult to the doctors who were supposed to be the experts.

Road safety works the same way. The UK's road fatality rate fell from around 7,500 deaths per year in the early 1970s to under 1,700 by 2020 (Department for Transport, 2021). That's a massive change — thousands of lives saved every year — achieved through a combination of seatbelt legislation, speed limits, drink-driving laws, road design improvements, and vehicle safety standards. Nobody gets credited for those deaths not happening. There are no ceremonies for the people who lobbied for seatbelt legislation in the 1960s and 1970s. There's no memorial to the engineers who designed safer road layouts. The work was real. The lives saved were real. The credit was essentially zero.

Y2K is a particularly clear case study in this dynamic. In the late 1990s, thousands of programmers and IT professionals worked for years to audit and fix systems that used two-digit year formats, which would have caused failures when clocks rolled over to 2000. When 1 January 2000 arrived and nothing catastrophic happened, the immediate cultural response was mockery. The whole thing had been overblown. The fears were ridiculous. The consultants had made a fortune out of nothing. What actually happened was that the nothing was the result of the work — but because it was a nothing, the work was retrospectively reinterpreted as unnecessary (Yourdon & Yourdon, 1998). Prevention, when it succeeds completely, looks like it was never needed.

In public health, vaccines are the canonical example. Vaccination eliminated smallpox, a disease that killed an estimated 300 million people in the 20th century alone (Fenner et al., 1988). Because smallpox no longer exists in the general population, it is now an abstract historical fact rather than a visceral present threat. The result is that the threat is systematically underestimated, and the preventive measure — vaccination — faces resistance that the original disease, were people still dying from it, would almost certainly not face. When prevention works well enough, it erases its own justification from people's lived experience.

The cognitive bias angle

The cognitive science underpinning this is fairly well-established. Beyond the availability heuristic, there's the related concept of negativity bias: we weight negative events more heavily than positive ones of equivalent magnitude (Baumeister et al., 2001). A near miss feels more significant than a routine safe journey. A contaminated water supply makes headlines; the years of safe water supply that preceded it don't. This asymmetry means that reactive responses to visible harm get more attention, more funding, and more political will than proactive responses to potential harm.

There's also an attribution problem. When something goes wrong, there's a clear causal chain: something happened, someone was responsible, here is the outcome. When something is prevented, the causal chain runs through a counterfactual. The traffic cone sits in the road, the driver swerves, no one is hurt. The cone is not credited for the non-crash. The person who placed it is not credited for the non-crash. The near-absence of injury is just the normal, unremarkable texture of a day that went fine.

In organisational settings, this creates what some management researchers call the "hero vs. guardian" dynamic (Taleb, 2012). The person who averts a crisis before it becomes visible is rarely rewarded. The person who responds to the crisis after it becomes visible often is — even if the crisis was preventable, even if the heroic response was only necessary because the preventive work wasn't done or wasn't funded. Nassim Taleb makes this point sharply in Antifragile: a politician who prevents a war gets none of the credit that a general who wins one receives, even though preventing it would have been the better outcome by any reasonable measure.

Prevention in everyday life

It's not just a macro-level phenomenon. It shows up in ordinary professional life constantly. The software engineer who catches a security vulnerability before deployment gets no fanfare — the system just keeps working. The one who fixes the breach after it happens gets war stories and a reputation. The editor who cuts the paragraph that would have caused a complaint is invisible; the editor who misses it gets the angry email. The accountant who spots the discrepancy before it becomes a problem gets a nod; the one who handles the audit after it became a problem gets a starring role.

I work in data science, and this dynamic comes up in subtle ways. A model that silently prevents bad recommendations — filtering out outputs that would have caused harm — is never celebrated, because the bad recommendations just don't appear. The model that generates the flashy result, the chart that gets into the boardroom presentation, the prediction that was dramatically right — that's what gets noticed. The invisible guard rail is structurally ignored by anyone who isn't specifically looking for it.

The same is true in personal life. Driving defensively — keeping space, anticipating what other drivers might do, slowing down before the situation demands it — means you avoid incidents that a less attentive driver would have had. But you can't point to those incidents, because they didn't happen. You just arrive safely, unremarkably, every time. There's no medal for that. The driver who reacts brilliantly in an emergency gets the story.

Why it matters that we get this wrong

The practical consequences are significant. Preventive health gets systematically underfunded relative to treatment. Mental health prevention receives a fraction of what mental health crisis intervention receives, despite evidence that early intervention is cheaper and more effective (Knapp et al., 2011). Infrastructure maintenance — the kind that prevents bridges from failing, water systems from contaminating, power grids from going down — is perennially deprioritised in favour of visible new-build projects, because maintenance preserves the status quo while new builds create visible evidence of activity and investment.

Pandemic preparedness is the most politically vivid example. After SARS in 2003 and H1N1 in 2009, funding for pandemic preparedness increased. As memories faded and the threat became abstract, funding was cut again. When COVID-19 hit, many governments were caught without stockpiles, without plans, without infrastructure, because the preparation had been allowed to atrophy. The people who had warned this would happen — who had written the reports, run the simulations, made the recommendations — were largely ignored until the moment that their predictions came true, at which point the crisis demanded a reactive response rather than the proactive one they had been advocating for (Gates, 2015).

This isn't irrational in the narrow sense. Politicians respond to visible constituents with visible needs. Voters punish inaction on visible problems and often don't reward proactive action on invisible ones. The incentive structures push systematically toward reaction and away from prevention. Knowing that the bias exists doesn't dissolve it.

What to do with this

There are a few reasonable responses. The first is just to make prevention visible — to actively count and communicate the counterfactual. Public health bodies that track "deaths averted" rather than just deaths, engineering teams that document near-misses and the interventions that prevented them, organisations that formally recognise the people who stopped problems rather than just the people who solved them. This doesn't eliminate the bias, but it creates some structure to work against it.

The second is to take seriously the expertise of people who are warning about things that haven't happened yet. That's a hard epistemological position to hold consistently, because it requires you to weigh expert predictions about invisible future harms against the visible present cost of acting on them. But the track record suggests that the cost of ignoring those warnings, when they're well-founded, is consistently higher than the cost of acting on them.

The third is personal: to actually notice and credit the preventive work around you. The colleague who read the brief carefully and caught the error. The parent who planned the route before the trip and avoided the traffic. The developer who wrote the test that caught the regression. These things have no drama. They often have no story. But they are the quiet majority of what makes things go well, and treating them as invisible — as the default, unremarkable texture of a competent life — undersells the effort behind them.

Geoffrey Rose (1985) put it well: the prevention paradox means that the visible beneficiaries of a mass preventive measure are few, while the hidden beneficiaries are many. That asymmetry doesn't mean the intervention wasn't worth it. It means the accounting is hard. It means you have to work a bit harder to see the value. But the value is there, in every crash that didn't happen, every outbreak that didn't spread, every disaster that didn't arrive.
